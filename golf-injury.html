Golf Injury & Swing Correction

One of Dr. Tanner's specialties. He is TPI Certified at Levels 1 and 2, developed the Golfer's Edge program for the Desert Mountain Golf Community, and has served as physician for PGA professionals. We treat the injury — and the swing fault that caused it.

What's Actually Going On

Golf is a high-velocity rotational sport. The body pays for what the swing can't deliver.

The modern golf swing produces clubhead speeds north of 100 mph and rotational forces measured in multiples of body weight. If any segment of the body can't produce its share of the motion, another segment compensates — and that segment is where the injury lives.

The most common golf injuries we treat:

  • Lumbar facet joint irritation and disc strain
  • Lead-side hip impingement and labral strain
  • Rotator cuff strain and impingement (lead shoulder)
  • Golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis) and tennis elbow
  • Wrist tendon injuries — lead wrist especially
  • Knee pain — lead knee from rotation, trail knee from instability
  • Thoracic spine stiffness and rib pain

Each of these usually traces back to a specific swing fault — early extension, reverse spine angle, loss of posture, casting, sway, slide — and to a specific body limitation that forced the swing to do it that way.

The TPI Approach

Body screen first. Swing change second.

The Titleist Performance Institute approach starts from a clinical premise: fix the body, and the swing will follow. Trying to coach a swing change when the body physically can't do the motion is how golfers get injured.

Dr. Tanner's TPI movement screen takes about 20 minutes and identifies which body limitations are forcing your swing into compensations. Common findings:

  • Limited hip internal rotation — drives early extension
  • Limited thoracic rotation — drives reverse spine angle
  • Limited ankle dorsiflexion — drives loss of posture
  • Weak glutes — drives sway, slide, and lower-back compensation
  • Poor pelvic separation — drives over-the-top swing path

Once we know which limitations are present, the treatment plan becomes specific — not generic stretches, not generic strengthening.
