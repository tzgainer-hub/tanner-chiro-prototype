Lower Back Pain

The most common condition we treat — and the one most often misdiagnosed as a single problem. Most lower back pain is the end result of three or four things going wrong together. Fixing it means addressing all of them.

What's Actually Going On

The lumbar spine is rarely the only culprit.

The lumbar spine has five vertebrae, intervertebral discs, facet joints, and dozens of muscles and ligaments that stabilize it. When something hurts, the source is usually a combination — not a single structure.

Typical contributors:

  • Core weakness — the deep abdominal and gluteal muscles aren't stabilizing the spine
  • Hip mobility loss — the hips don't move, so the lumbar spine moves too much instead
  • Disc bulge or degeneration — pressure on a nerve root or the disc itself
  • Facet joint dysfunction — the small joints at the back of the spine get stuck or inflamed
  • SI (sacroiliac) joint imbalance — pelvic asymmetry shifts load through the lumbar spine
  • Postural compensation — desk work, driving, prior injury

This is why a single treatment — just adjustments, just stretches, just rest — usually doesn't hold. The pain comes back because the cause didn't get addressed.

When To Come In

Signs your back pain shouldn't wait.

  • Pain that has lasted more than 7–10 days without improvement
  • Pain that radiates down one leg (this is sciatica — see our sciatica page)
  • Pain that wakes you at night or interrupts sleep
  • Stiffness so severe you can't bend forward to tie your shoes
  • Episodes that keep coming back — same pain every few months
  • New pain after a fall, lift, twist, or athletic incident

If you have numbness in the saddle area, loss of bowel or bladder function, or sudden severe weakness in a leg, that's an emergency room visit, not a chiropractor visit.

How We Treat It

The integrated plan — usually 4 to 8 weeks.

Treatment starts with a real exam — orthopedic, neurological, and movement-based — to identify which structures are involved. From there, the plan combines several of these:

Step 1

Chiropractic Adjustment

Restores joint motion in the lumbar spine and SI joint. Relieves the immediate inflammation and muscle guarding. The starting point — but rarely the entire plan.

If discs are involved

Spinal Decompression

Targeted lumbar traction creates negative pressure inside the disc — pulling nucleus material back from the nerve root. Drug-free relief for herniated and bulging discs.

For muscle spasm

Soft Tissue & Massage

Release of the paraspinal, QL, glute, and piriformis muscles that lock down around an inflamed spine. Coordinated with your adjustment schedule for maximum effect.

The fix-the-cause piece

Corrective Exercise

Build the core stability, glute strength, and hip mobility that the spine has been compensating for. This is what keeps the pain from coming back after 6 months.

Strength & Return-to-Activity

Personal Training

Once the acute phase is past, structured strength work is what rebuilds the resilience the spine needs. Programs are written around your specific weaknesses and the activity you want to get back to.

Return to function

Clinical Pilates

Reformer-based, clinically programmed Pilates rebuilds spinal stability and movement quality. Excellent for active adults rebuilding after the acute phase.

A Note On Surgery

Most lower back pain doesn't need surgery — and most surgeons agree.

Modern spine surgeons routinely refer patients to conservative care first. For non-emergency cases (no neurological deficit, no acute trauma), 6–12 weeks of integrated conservative treatment is the standard recommendation before surgical evaluation. We work alongside that timeline — and if at the end you still need surgery, you'll go in stronger and recover faster.
