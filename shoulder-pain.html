Shoulder & Rotator Cuff Pain

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body — and the most dependent on the scapula, thoracic spine, and rotator cuff working together. When shoulder pain shows up, the cause is rarely just the shoulder.

What's Actually Going On

Shoulder pain is almost always biomechanical — not just structural.

The shoulder is a shallow ball-and-socket joint held in place mostly by soft tissue. Range of motion depends on the scapula sliding correctly across the upper back, the thoracic spine extending and rotating, and the rotator cuff coordinating with the larger shoulder muscles. If any of those break down, the shoulder pays.

The most common shoulder problems we treat:

  • Rotator cuff strain or tear — overuse, athletic loading, age-related degeneration
  • Shoulder impingement — the rotator cuff getting pinched under the AC joint with overhead motion
  • Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis) — progressive loss of range of motion, often in 50+ patients
  • AC joint sprain or arthritis — the joint at the top of the shoulder where collarbone meets shoulder blade
  • Biceps tendinitis — irritation of the long head of the biceps tendon
  • Referred pain from the neck — cervical radiculopathy that masquerades as shoulder pain
  • Postural shoulder pain — forward, rounded posture loading the shoulder incorrectly

The first job is to figure out which of these (often more than one) is producing the pain.

When To Come In

Signs your shoulder shouldn't wait.

  • Pain that wakes you when you sleep on that side
  • Difficulty reaching overhead — putting away dishes, hanging clothes
  • Difficulty reaching behind your back — fastening a bra, reaching a back pocket
  • Weakness lifting groceries or a gallon of milk
  • Pain in the shoulder that radiates down the arm or into the neck
  • Clicking, grinding, or catching with shoulder motion
  • A recent fall, lift, or athletic incident that hasn't resolved in 5–7 days

Sudden severe weakness, deformity, or inability to lift the arm after a fall = urgent care or ER, not chiropractor.

How We Treat It

Treat the cuff, the joint, the upper back, and the swing pattern that caused it.

A shoulder plan almost never involves just the shoulder. Treatment usually combines several of these:

Foundation

Thoracic & Shoulder Adjustment

Targeted adjustments to the thoracic spine, ribs, and shoulder complex. Most shoulder dysfunction has a stuck upper back component that has to be released.

For Cervical-Referred Pain

Decompression Therapy

Many "shoulder" problems are actually cervical radiculopathy — pain referred down from the neck. Decompression therapy unloads the cervical nerve root so the shoulder symptoms resolve.

For Soft Tissue Lock-Up

Massage Therapy

Direct release of the rotator cuff, deltoid, levator scapulae, and pec minor. Critical for impingement and frozen shoulder cases.

For Inflammation

Cold Laser Therapy

Reduces inflammation in the rotator cuff and bursa. Particularly useful in the acute phase to accelerate recovery.

For Posture & Movement

Clinical Pilates

Reformer-based work rebuilds scapular control and thoracic mobility. The shoulder can only sit correctly when the upper back and rib cage are doing their job.

The Cause Fix

Rotator Cuff & Scapular Rehab

Retraining the scapular control and rotator cuff strength that has to hold the shoulder in proper position. This is what keeps the pain from coming back.
