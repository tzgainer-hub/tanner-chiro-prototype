Sciatica

Here is the single most important thing to understand: sciatica is not a diagnosis. It is a symptom — pain along the path of the sciatic nerve. Treating it well means identifying which of three or four underlying causes is producing it.

What's Actually Going On

The sciatic nerve runs from the lower back to the foot. Several things can compress it.

The sciatic nerve is formed by the lumbar and sacral nerve roots, exits the pelvis, runs underneath (or sometimes through) the piriformis muscle, and travels down the back of the leg. Compression or irritation anywhere along that path can produce "sciatica."

The real causes we have to distinguish between:

  • Lumbar disc herniation or bulge — disc material pressing on a nerve root at L4/L5 or L5/S1. The most common cause in patients over 30.
  • Piriformis syndrome — the piriformis muscle in the buttock compressing the sciatic nerve as it exits the pelvis. Common in runners, cyclists, and sitters.
  • SI (sacroiliac) joint dysfunction — the joint between the sacrum and pelvis is inflamed or misaligned, producing a sciatica-like radiation.
  • Lumbar facet irritation — the small joints at the back of the spine refer pain in a sciatica pattern.
  • Spinal stenosis — narrowing of the spinal canal, more common in patients over 60.

Each of these has a different treatment plan. The exam tells us which one (or which combination) we're dealing with.

When To Come In

Signs your sciatica shouldn't wait.

  • Pain that radiates below the knee or into the foot
  • Numbness, tingling, or "pins and needles" down the leg
  • Weakness in the leg or foot — trouble lifting the foot, weakness on stairs
  • Pain that's worse when you sit than when you stand
  • Pain that's been going on for more than 7–10 days
  • Pain that wakes you at night

Go to the emergency room if you have loss of bladder or bowel function, sudden severe weakness in either leg, or numbness in the saddle area (groin/inner thighs). This is cauda equina syndrome — a surgical emergency.

How We Treat It

Diagnosis first. Then the plan that matches the source.

The first visit includes a neurological and orthopedic exam to determine which of the causes is producing your symptoms. Treatment is then matched to the source.

For Disc Cases

Spinal Decompression

Targeted lumbar traction creates negative pressure inside the disc, drawing nucleus material back away from the nerve root. The drug-free, surgery-free first-line for disc herniation.

For Joint Causes

Lumbar & SI Adjustment

For facet and SI joint sources, targeted adjustments restore motion and reduce nerve irritation. Often produces meaningful relief in the first 1–2 visits.

For Piriformis Cases

Soft Tissue & Trigger Point

Direct release of the piriformis, glute, and hip rotator muscles that compress the sciatic nerve as it exits the pelvis. Combined with corrective exercise for the underlying glute weakness.

For Inflammation

Cold Laser Therapy

Reduces inflammation around the nerve root. Particularly useful in the first 2–3 weeks of an acute flare-up to accelerate recovery.

For Tendon Source

Shock Wave Therapy

For chronic glute and hamstring tendon involvement that hasn't responded to other care. Has helped patients avoid escalation to injection.

The Cause Fix

Corrective Exercise

Build the glute strength, hip mobility, and core stability that the spine has been compensating for. This is what stops the sciatica from coming back.

Considering Surgery?

Most sciatica resolves with conservative care — including most disc-related cases.

Modern spine surgeons routinely refer disc-related sciatica to a 6–12 week course of conservative care before surgical evaluation. Spinal decompression therapy has helped many of our patients avoid surgery entirely. If at the end you still need surgery, you'll go in with a stronger core and a clearer picture of what worked and what didn't.
